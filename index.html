📈2. Market Maturity

Is customer acquisition cost low?
Yes CAC is low compared to industry benchmarks and much lower than your customer lifetime value (CLV). Indicates highly efficient, scalable, and sustainable acquisition.
Moderate CAC is in line with industry averages and/or your CLV is only moderately higher than CAC. Indicates average acquisition efficiency.
No CAC is high compared to industry or close to/higher than your CLV. Indicates acquisition is costly and may not be sustainable.
Are customers aware of this solution?
Yes High awareness: Most target customers know about the solution through marketing, word-of-mouth, or industry presence. Brand/message is recognized and recall is strong.
Somewhat Moderate awareness: Some customers are aware, but many are not. Awareness may be limited to certain segments, channels, or regions.
No Low awareness: Target customers are generally unaware of the solution. Brand/message has little to no recognition in the market.
Is adoption timeline short (<6 months)?
Yes Most customers adopt and deploy the solution within 6 months of first contact or purchase. Indicates low barriers to adoption and rapid onboarding.
Moderate Adoption typically takes 6–12 months. Some onboarding or change management is required, but not excessive.
No Adoption usually takes more than 12 months. Indicates high complexity, long sales cycles, or significant barriers to customer onboarding.
Does robust infrastructure exist?
Yes All critical systems and processes are in place, reliable, and scalable. Infrastructure is well-documented, monitored, and can support current and future needs with minimal risk.
Partially Some core infrastructure exists and is functional, but there are gaps in reliability, scalability, or documentation. May need upgrades or improvements for full robustness.
No Infrastructure is insufficient, unreliable, or missing key components. Major upgrades or new systems are required to support operations or growth.
Is the product intuitive and easy to use?
Yes The product is intuitive and easy to use; most customers require little to no training or onboarding to achieve success.
Somewhat Some customer education is needed (e.g., onboarding, tutorials, or support), but most users can get started with moderate effort.
No Significant customer education is required; users need extensive training or support to use the product effectively.

What is the current TRL level?
Can it be manufactured at scale?
Yes, proven at commercial scale The product/process has already been manufactured reliably and cost-effectively at commercial scale using existing facilities, suppliers, and processes.
Potentially, with moderate challenges Manufacturing at scale is feasible but requires some process adjustments, investment, or supply chain development. No major technical barriers foreseen.
No, significant barriers remain Major technical, cost, or supply chain challenges exist; manufacturing at scale is not currently feasible or proven.
No specialized materials required?
Yes The product can be manufactured using standard, widely available materials and components—no rare, proprietary, or hard-to-source materials are needed.
No Specialized, rare, or proprietary materials are required, which may increase cost, risk, or supply chain complexity.
Does it outperform alternatives?
Yes, significantly The solution delivers clear, measurable, and important advantages over all key alternatives (e.g., better performance, cost, usability, or features) that customers value.
Somewhat The solution is on par with or slightly better than alternatives in some areas, but not all; advantages may not be compelling for all customers.
No The solution does not outperform alternatives in meaningful ways, or may even underperform on key criteria.
Does it offer significant advantages?
Yes The solution provides clear, important, and valuable benefits over existing products or methods—such as better performance, cost savings, or unique features that matter to customers.
No The solution does not provide significant or meaningful advantages compared to alternatives; benefits are minor or not valued by customers.
Are technical limitations minimal?
Yes There are few or no technical barriers to implementation or scaling. The technology is robust, reliable, and does not require significant further development or risk mitigation.
No There are significant technical challenges, unresolved issues, or risks that could hinder implementation, scaling, or long-term reliability.
Minimal technical risks?
Yes, minimal Only minor technical risks remain. The technology is mature, well-tested, and unlikely to face major failures or setbacks.
Moderate Some technical risks exist, but they are manageable and mitigation plans are in place. Technology may need further testing or refinement.
No, significant risks Significant technical risks remain unresolved. There is a high chance of failure, major rework, or unexpected technical barriers.

⚔️4. Competition

Few or no strong competitors?
Yes There are few or no strong competitors in the market; your offering is unique or faces only weak, fragmented, or indirect competition. This increases your potential for market share and pricing power.
No There are several strong, established competitors targeting the same customers with similar offerings; significant rivalry exists and differentiation is challenging.
Competitors have <30% market share?
Yes No single competitor controls more than 30% of the total market. The market is fragmented, reducing the risk of dominance and making it easier to gain share.
No One or more competitors control 30% or more of the total market. The market is concentrated, and dominant players may have significant pricing power and influence.
Are alternative solutions weak?
Yes Competing or substitute solutions are limited in capability, outdated, poorly adopted, or do not fully meet customer needs. Your offering has a clear edge.
No Alternative solutions are strong, widely used, or effectively meet customer needs—making it differentiation and adoption more challenging.
Low threat from emerging alternatives?
Yes There are few or no credible emerging technologies or solutions that could disrupt or replace this technology in the near future, indicating a stable competitive environment.
No Emerging alternatives pose a significant risk of disruption or replacement, potentially undermining the technology's market position or viability.
Clear unique selling proposition?
Yes, clear and compelling The product/service has a unique, well-defined, and easily communicated selling proposition that is highly relevant and valuable to the target market, and clearly differentiates it from competitors.
Somewhat There is some differentiation, but the unique selling proposition is not fully clear, compelling, or consistently communicated; may overlap with competitors in some areas.
No The product/service lacks a clear or meaningful unique selling proposition; it is difficult to distinguish from competitors in the eyes of customers.
Are advantages sustainable?
Yes The business has advantages (e.g., technology, brand, process, relationships) that are durable and hard for competitors to copy or erode over time. This provides a lasting edge in the market.
No Advantages are easily replicated, temporary, or likely to be lost as competitors catch up, new technologies emerge, or market conditions change.
Hard to identify and reverse engineer?
Yes The technology's design, architecture, or code is highly complex, proprietary, or protected—making it very difficult for competitors to analyze, copy, or reproduce through reverse engineering or deconstruction.
No The technology can be readily analyzed, understood, or copied by competitors using standard reverse engineering techniques, making it vulnerable to imitation.

📜5. Patentability / IP Strength

No existing patents block commercialization?
Yes, freedom to operate is clear No active patents or IP rights block commercialization in your target markets. You have full freedom to operate and can launch, make, use, or sell the product without risk of infringement.
Partially, minor risks or licensing needed Most major patent barriers are cleared, but there may be minor risks or the need for limited licensing/cross-licensing. No major blocking patents, but some diligence required.
No, blocking patents exist One or more active patents block commercialization in key markets. Launch would risk infringement, require major licensing, or legal workarounds.
Is the technology novel, inventive and applicable?
Yes, fully meets all criteria The technology is new (not previously made public), involves a non-obvious inventive step, and can be used in industry or practical applications. Meets all main patentability standards.
Partially—some criteria met The technology is new or inventive, but may have questions about obviousness or practical applicability. Some aspects are strong, but not all criteria are clearly met.
No, does not meet key criteria The technology is not new, is an obvious variation, or cannot be practically applied. Does not meet the requirements for patentability.
Are multiple forms of IP protection available (patents, trade secrets, copyright)?
Yes, multiple forms available The technology can be protected by more than one type of IP (e.g., patents, trade secrets, copyright, and/or trademarks), allowing for overlapping or complementary protection and stronger legal defense.
Only one form available Only a single type of IP protection (e.g., just a patent or just a trade secret) is realistically available for this technology.
No, not eligible for IP protection The technology cannot be effectively protected by patents, trade secrets, copyright, or other recognized forms of IP.
Sufficient geographic scope of protection?
Yes IP rights (patents, trademarks, etc.) are secured in all key markets and regions where commercialization or enforcement is planned. This broad coverage reduces risk of infringement and supports global growth.
No IP protection is limited to a few countries or regions, or does not cover all major markets, leaving the technology exposed to competition or infringement elsewhere.
High barriers to entry due to IP?
Yes, very high Your IP (patents, trademarks, copyrights, or trade secrets) provides strong, enforceable exclusivity, making it extremely difficult and costly for competitors to enter or operate in your market. This confers significant market power and protection.
Moderate Your IP provides some barriers to entry, but workarounds or alternative approaches are possible. Competitors may be able to enter the market with effort or by innovating around your IP.
No, low barriers Your IP does not provide substantial barriers; competitors can enter the market easily, either because IP protection is weak, unenforceable, or alternatives are readily available.
